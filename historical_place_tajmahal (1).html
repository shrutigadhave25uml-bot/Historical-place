What is the Taj Mahal?

The Taj Mahal is an immense ivory-white marble mausoleum located on the south bank of the Yamuna River in Agra, India. Widely regarded as one of the greatest architectural achievements in human history, it stands as a timeless symbol of love, loss, and imperial grandeur. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Commissioned by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died in 1631 during childbirth, the monument took over two decades to complete. Its breathtaking symmetry, intricate inlay work, and serene gardens make it an unparalleled masterpiece that continues to enchant millions of visitors every year.

Location & History

The Taj Mahal is situated in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India, approximately 200 km south of New Delhi. Construction began in 1632 CE under Emperor Shah Jahan and was completed around 1653 CE. It took the labour of over 20,000 artisans, craftsmen, and architects from across the Mughal Empire and beyond, including Persia, Ottoman Turkey, and Europe.

The chief architect is believed to be Ustad Ahmad Lahori. Materials were sourced from all over India and Central Asia — white marble from Makrana, Rajasthan; jasper from Punjab; jade and crystal from China; turquoise from Tibet; and sapphire from Sri Lanka. The result is one of the finest examples of Mughal architecture, blending Persian, Islamic, and Indian styles.

Historical & Cultural Importance

The Taj Mahal is not merely a tomb — it is a testament to the heights of Mughal civilisation and its contribution to world art and architecture. Shah Jahan reportedly wept each day at the sight of it after Mumtaz Mahal's death, channelling his grief into creating something immortal.

It has inspired poets, painters, and rulers for centuries. Rabindranath Tagore described it as "a teardrop on the cheek of time." The complex includes the main mausoleum, a mosque, a guest house, and the magnificent Charbagh — a symmetrical paradise garden divided by waterways. The entire complex was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and has been recognised as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World since 2007.

Interesting Facts

  • The Taj Mahal appears to change colour — pinkish at dawn, white at noon, and golden at night.
  • It took over 20,000 workers and 1,000 elephants to transport materials during construction.
  • The four minarets lean slightly outward to protect the main dome in case of earthquakes.
  • Shah Jahan was later imprisoned by his own son Aurangzeb and could only view the Taj from his cell.
  • The marble is inlaid with over 28 types of precious and semi-precious stones.
  • The calligraphy inscribed on the arches is designed to appear uniform despite its varying heights.
  • Legend says Shah Jahan planned a black marble twin tomb across the river, but it was never built.
  • The Taj Mahal was camouflaged with scaffolding during World War II to protect it from air raids.
  • The main dome is 73 metres (240 ft) high — roughly the height of a 24-storey building.
  • It is one of the most photographed monuments in the world, attracting ~40,000 visitors daily.
